## High-impact JAMA / NEJM / Nature commentary

### A Nationwide Network of Health AI Assurance Laboratories
Shah NH, Halamka JD, Saria S, Pencina M, Tazbaz T, Tripathi M, Callahan
A, Hildahl H, Anderson B. *JAMA* 331(3):245-249 (January 16, 2024).
DOI: 10.1001/jama.2023.26930. PMID: 38117493.

**Core argument:** The U.S. needs a federated public-private network of
"assurance labs" to continuously evaluate health AI. No publicly
available, nationwide mechanism currently exists. Not a single central
agency — federalism, practically and politically distinctive.

**Stead/Aliferis reply (JAMA March 2024):** pushed back that AI should
follow staged-trial structure like drugs. Shah/Halamka/Anderson accepted
the analogy and reframed assurance labs as the mechanism for those
stages. Characteristic Shah move: concede the framing, co-opt the
critique.

### Avoiding Financial Toxicity for Patients from Clinicians' Use of AI
Jain SS, Mello MM, Shah NH. *NEJM* 391(13):1171-1173 (October 3, 2024).
DOI: 10.1056/NEJMp2406135. PMID: 39348681.

**Core argument:** AI harms in medicine won't just be diagnostic errors
— they'll be economic. When AI recommends confirmatory testing, the
clinician documents and orders it, the patient gets the bill. Existing
insurance frameworks don't yet distinguish AI-originated vs.
physician-originated recommendations.

### AI in Health and Health Care: Priorities for Action
Matheny ME, ... Shah NH et al. *Health Affairs* 44(2) (January 22,
2025). NAM Vital Directions 2025. DOI: 10.1377/hlthaff.2024.01003.

**Core argument:** Four national strategic priorities: (1) safe,
effective, trustworthy use, (2) AI-competent workforce, (3) research
funding for delivery science, (4) liability and responsibility
clarification. Positioning: the document that briefed the incoming
U.S. presidential administration.

### Creation and Adoption of Large Language Models in Medicine
Shah NH, Entwistle D, Pfeffer MA. *JAMA* 330(9):866-869 (September 5,
2023). DOI: 10.1001/jama.2023.14217. PMID: 37548965. **~470 citations.**

**Core argument:** LLMs are being deployed for medical tasks without
(a) being trained on medical records and (b) any verification that they
benefit the tasks they're being used for. Three actions: provision
relevant training data, specify desired benefits, evaluate benefits in
real-world deployments.

**Institutional weight:** co-authored with Stanford Health Care's CEO
(David Entwistle) and CMIO (Michael Pfeffer).

### Testing and Evaluation of Health Care Applications of Large Language Models: A Systematic Review
Bedi S, Liu Y, ... Shah NH. *JAMA* 333(4):319-328 (January 28, 2025).
PMID: 39405325. **~518 citations within a year of publication.**

**The empirical backbone of his "LLM evaluation is broken" argument:**
systematic review of 519 studies (Jan 2022 – Feb 2024). **Only 5% used
real patient care data.** Accuracy dominated; fairness, bias, and
toxicity under-studied.

### Catalyzing Health AI by Fixing Payment Systems
Razavian N, Batchu-Green P, Chowdhry V, Elemento O, Rajpurkar P, Saria
S, Shah NH, Topol EJ. *NEJM AI* (November 24, 2025). DOI:
10.1056/AIpc2500871. PMID: 41695240.

**Notable coalition:** Shah, Topol, Saria, Rajpurkar on one paper. Rare
alignment.

**Core argument:** Reimbursement is the bottleneck, not the algorithm.
FDA-cleared AI tools face catastrophic adoption barriers because CPT
code adoption hasn't caught up.

### Ensuring useful adoption of generative artificial intelligence in healthcare
Jindal JA, Lungren MP, Shah NH. *JAMIA* 31(6):1441-1444 (May 20, 2024).
PMID: 38452298.

**Signature finding:** **Top-down GenAI deployments in health systems
produce unclear value; bottom-up adoption is more valuable.**
Culture-fit argument.

## Foundational ethical / humanism pieces

### What This Computer Needs Is a Physician: Humanism and Artificial Intelligence
Verghese A, Shah NH, Harrington RA. *JAMA* 319(1):19-20 (2018).
DOI: 10.1001/jama.2017.19198. **~550 citations.**

Co-authored with Abraham Verghese (Stanford's humanist-physician figure).
Preserves collegial rituals and the physical exam. "The two cultures —
computer and the physician — must work together."

### Implementing Machine Learning in Health Care — Addressing Ethical Challenges
Char DS, Shah NH, Magnus D. *NEJM* 378(11):981-983 (March 15, 2018).
**~2,268 citations.**

The go-to citation for ethics of clinical ML. Shah is middle author, but
the paper is one of his most-cited career works.

### Making Machine Learning Models Clinically Useful
Shah NH, Milstein A, Bagley SC. *JAMA* 322(14):1351-1352 (October 8,
2019). DOI: 10.1001/jama.2019.10306. PMID: 31393527.

Plants decision-curve / net-benefit thinking in clinical ML: utility
evaluated against *range of clinical decisions available, cost and
efficacy, and patient adherence probability*.

## Signature positions (synthesis)

1. A model is not a product — the care-delivery system is the product.
2. All models are local — against one-time external validation.
3. Prove the benefit, don't assume it — evaluation must use real
   clinical data, not exam questions.
4. If it's worth deploying, it's worth simulating financially and
   ethically first — FURM as gate.
5. Bedside evidence beats guideline evidence when the patient doesn't
   fit the guideline — Green Button thesis.
6. Top-down AI mandates fail; culture-fit bottom-up adoption works.
7. AI harms aren't just clinical; they're financial.
8. Assurance is a national infrastructure problem, not a vendor problem.
9. Usefulness has quantitative operationalization — simulate it (APLUS).
10. The academic-to-deployment timeline is a decade, and that's okay.
