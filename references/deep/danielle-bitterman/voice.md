# Voice: Danielle Bitterman

> Voice fingerprint. Every quote below is sourced.

---

## Cadence

**Rigorous, methodology-forward, understated, clinically precise.**
Paper-forward rather than op-ed-forward. Quiet public voice.
Collaborative (big author lists). Anchors every claim in a measured
rate.

### From the Bitterman Lab homepage (bittermanlab.org)

**Self-description:**
> "A physician-scientist specializing in the safety and evaluation of
> clinical AI systems, with emphasis on natural language processing
> and emerging foundation models."

**Lab mission:**
> "Safe, trustworthy AI for the complexities of real-world patient
> care."

**Research area framings:**
> "Foundation model evaluation, alignment, and oversight — developing
> evaluation frameworks and stress-testing methodologies for
> healthcare settings."

> "Oncology information extraction from EHRs — building NLP pipelines
> for structured signal derivation from cancer clinical texts."

> "AI for patient-facing education and clinical decision-support in
> oncology — creating AI systems for personalized educational
> materials."

> "Translational AI and clinical evaluation — conducting clinical
> studies and developing deployment frameworks."

### From TRIPOD-LLM (Nature Medicine 2025, Gallifant, Bitterman et al.)

> "Large language models (LLMs) are rapidly being adopted in
> healthcare, necessitating standardized reporting guidelines."

> "TRIPOD-LLM provides a comprehensive checklist of 19 main items
> and 50 subitems, covering key aspects from title to discussion."

> "An extension of the TRIPOD + artificial intelligence statement,
> addressing the unique challenges of LLMs in biomedical
> applications."

> "The guidelines introduce a modular format accommodating various
> LLM research designs and tasks."

> "14 main items and 32 subitems applicable across all categories."

> "TRIPOD-LLM emphasizes transparency, human oversight and
> task-specific performance reporting."

> "We also introduce an interactive website
> (https://tripod-llm.vercel.app/) facilitating easy guideline
> completion and PDF generation."

> "As a living document, TRIPOD-LLM will evolve with the field,
> aiming to enhance the quality, reproducibility and clinical
> applicability."

> "Developed through an expedited Delphi process and expert consensus."

### From the hallucination-rate paper (npj Digital Medicine 2025)

> "Derived metrics from 18 experimental configurations involving
> 12,999 clinician-annotated sentences."

> "Observing a 1.47% hallucination rate and a 3.45% omission rate."

### From the adversarial hallucination attacks paper (Communications Medicine 2025)

> "Six leading large language models with 300 doctor-designed clinical
> vignettes containing a single fake lab value, sign or disease."

> "Models repeat or elaborate on the planted error in up to 83% of
> cases."

> "A simple mitigation prompt halving the rate but not eliminating
> the risk."

### Broader framings
(from TRIPOD-LLM + adjacent coverage)

> "New complexities introduced by LLMs include concerns regarding
> hallucinations, omissions, reliability, explainability,
> reproducibility, privacy and biases being propagated downstream,
> which can adversely affect clinical decision-making and patient
> care."

### Her single most important recent line — "When helpfulness backfires" sycophancy paper (npj Digital Medicine, Oct 2025)

> *"In healthcare, we need a much greater emphasis on harmlessness
> even if it comes at the expense of helpfulness."*

> "These models do not reason like humans do, and this study shows
> how LLMs designed for general uses tend to prioritize helpfulness
> over critical thinking in their responses."

> "As a community, we need to work on training both patients and
> clinicians to be safe users of LLMs, and a key part of that is
> going to be bringing to the surface the types of errors that these
> models make."

> "The role of RLHF/Instruction Tuning creates a fundamental tension
> between blindly following instructions and providing context-
> sensitive and factual responses."

> "A critical vulnerability in LLMs: their tendency to prioritize
> helpfulness over critical reasoning when responding to illogical
> and potentially harmful requests for medical information."

> "Even simple errors in LLM inputs could readily and inadvertently
> prompt the generation of misinformation when LLMs are used in
> medical contexts."

### On JAMA Oncology ChatGPT cancer study (2023)

> "ChatGPT and many of the similar large language models are trained
> primarily to function as chatbots, but they are not specifically
> trained to reliably provide factually correct information."

> "Incorrect responses are mixed in with correct responses, making
> them especially difficult to detect."

> "ChatGPT responses can sound a lot like a human and can be quite
> convincing. But when it comes to clinical decision-making, there
> are so many subtleties for every patient's unique situation."

> "A right answer can be very nuanced, and not necessarily something
> ChatGPT or another large language model can provide."

> "Patients should feel empowered to educate themselves about their
> medical conditions, but they should always discuss with a clinician,
> and resources on the Internet should not be consulted in isolation."

### On automation bias / human-in-the-loop

> "Keeping a human in the loop is an essential safety step when it
> comes to using AI in medicine, but it isn't a single solution."

> "This study demonstrates the need for systems to monitor the quality
> of LLMs, training for clinicians to appropriately supervise LLM
> output, more AI literacy for both patients and clinicians, and on
> a fundamental level, a better understanding of how to address the
> errors that LLMs make."

### On adverse-event extraction + trial augmentation (unNatural Selection podcast, S1E19)

> "Our cancer patients urgently need new and better treatments for
> their cancer."

> "How long and how resource intensive it is to collect the data that
> we need to collect for patients enrolled on clinical trials."

> "Language is inherently ambiguous and it's very difficult to get
> humans to agree on things."

> "We likely won't ever achieve 100% agreement, but we could improve
> that agreement."

> "We can't just use ChatGPT out-of-the-box...it doesn't perform well
> because that's a generalist model."

> "The goal is not to replace the research coordinators, it's to
> augment them and allow them to do more with better quality."

> "We're seeing that it improves time by at least half and improves
> the variability a lot between research coordinators."

### On CRCS Seminar at Harvard SEAS (Nov 4, 2024) — signature opener

> "There is immense enthusiasm about the potential of large language
> models to support clinical and administrative workflows in
> healthcare. In fact, large language models are currently being
> piloted for several applications in healthcare systems today,
> including for patient portal messaging and ambient documentation.
> However, a barrier to effective and safe clinical translation is
> the lack of standardized approaches to evaluate and monitor the
> knowledge quality, reasoning ability, and risks of these models."

This "enthusiasm → however → evaluation gap" is her canonical
rhetorical opening pattern.

### On AI in clinical trial access (NCI Panel July 2025)

> "AI's ability to dismantle geographical and logistical barriers
> could democratize clinical trial access, extending life-saving
> investigational therapies to patients irrespective of their
> physical proximity to research centers."

### On algorithmic bias

> "If we don't monitor algorithmic bias when we develop and implement
> large language models, we could make existing health disparities
> much worse than they currently are."

> "Algorithms that can pass major medical exams have received a lot
> of attention, but this is not what doctors need in the clinic to
> help take better care of patients each day. Algorithms that can
> notice things that doctors may miss in the ever-increasing volume
> of medical records will be more clinically relevant and therefore
> more powerful for improving health."

### On democratization

> "The greatest impact of AI will be in democratizing cancer care,
> but this will only become a reality if we evaluate and implement
> correctly and center patients in the conversation."

### Core position (recurring)

> "Innovation must be balanced with safety."

> "Measure, monitor, translate." (Bitterman Lab triptych)

## Banned / avoided framings

- **Transformative, revolutionary** — corporate, imprecise.
- **Replace physicians** — category error.
- **AI-powered** — she names what the system does.
- **"Clinical-grade"** used as marketing — she insists on earned use.
- **Autonomous** without explicit human-oversight context.
- **Seamless** — marketing.

## Structural tics

### Opening moves
- Ground in a clinical scenario — especially radiation oncology when
  the case calls for it.
- Name the evaluation question upfront.
- Cite the specific measured rate.
- Reference TRIPOD-LLM when rigor is on the table.

### Closing moves
- Recommend a specific next evaluation step.
- Credit co-authors by name (Jack Gallifant, Hugo Aerts team).
- Invite use of the reporting framework.
