# Positions & Tensions: Jonathan H. Chen

> Strong positions, his signature "no," predictions, collaborators.

---

## 1. The fundamental theorem — and its crack

**His longest-standing prior:**
> "I have, for years, restated the fundamental theorem of biomedical
> informatics that human + computer will outperform either alone."
> — Stanford FSI (2025)

**His own RCTs challenged that prior:**
> "It was thus startling when we did a series of studies on complex
> medical diagnostic and management reasoning problems and found that
> the computer — GPT-4 in this case — performed as well as or better
> than human physicians."

> "It begs uncomfortable questions about what the point of the
> human-in-the-loop is in the process if they are not substantially
> helping, and maybe even hindering, what the computer could do on
> its own."

**His response is not to retract but to re-pose:**
> "For years I've said that, when combined, human plus computer is
> going to do better than either one by itself."

> "I think this study challenges us to think about that more
> critically and ask ourselves, 'What is a computer good at? What is
> a human good at?'"

> "We may need to rethink where we use and combine those skills and
> for which tasks we recruit AI."

> "Perhaps it's a point in AI's favor."

**The 2026 redemption arc:** "From Tool to Teammate" (npj Digital
Medicine 2026). When collaboration is deliberately **designed** (not
just "put LLM next to doctor"), clinicians measurably improve over
traditional tools.

---

## 2. Don't skip the doctor

**Hard no on DIY chatbot medicine** (Stanford Medicine coverage of the
2025 Nature Medicine RCT):

> "This doesn't mean patients should skip the doctor and go straight
> to chatbots. Don't do that."

> "There's a lot of good information out there, but there's also bad
> information. The skill we all have to develop is discerning what's
> credible and what's not right. That's more important now than ever."

---

## 3. "Auto-complete on steroids — not arbiters of medical truth"

His most-quoted phrase. Reused across HIMSS, Healthcare IT News,
Stanford FSI, Scope blog. It's a 2017 NEJM descendant:

> "Machine learning, data analytics, they're very good at predicting
> things. But just because you can predict something doesn't mean you
> can get how to change it."

> "Many of the predictions that come out of systems can be highly
> accurate, but their accuracy may be based on things that were dumb
> obvious to a doctor anyway."

---

## 4. Prospective > retrospective evaluation

His methodological signature. Canonical trials:

1. **2024 JAMA Netw Open** — GPT-4 diagnostic RCT (50 physicians).
2. **2025 Nature Medicine** — GPT-4 management RCT (92 physicians).
3. **2026 npj Digital Medicine** — "From Tool to Teammate"
   collaboration RCT (70 clinicians).

The field has too many retrospective benchmark papers. He publishes
trials.

---

## 5. Wayfinding > label prediction

**His most portable philosophical concept.**

> "Current AI tools have not realized this potential, due in part to
> the long-standing focus of these tools on predicting final diagnostic
> labels instead of helping clinicians navigate the dynamic refinement
> process of diagnosis."

> "A new generation of AI is needed that considers the dynamism of
> the diagnostic process and answers the questions of where are the
> clinician and patient on the diagnostic pathway and what should be
> done next."

Applied to note generation: the AI isn't there to announce the
diagnosis; it's there to help the clinician **orient, refine, monitor.**

---

## 6. Chatbots say things. Agents do things.

**Chen's MedAgentBench framing** (Stanford HAI coverage):

> *"Chatbots say things. AI agents can do things."*

> "This means they could theoretically directly retrieve patient
> information from the electronic medical record, reason about that
> information, and take action by directly entering in orders for
> tests and medications. This is a much higher bar for autonomy in
> the high-stakes world of medical care."

> "We need a benchmark to establish the current state of AI capability
> on reproducible tasks that we can optimize toward."

The verb *"theoretically"* is doing heavy lifting. He's not endorsing
autonomous clinical agents — he's building the evaluation harness that
forces the claim to be earned.

---

## 7. Bedside workflow realism — the chart at 3am

From the ChatEHR launch (Stanford Medicine, June 2025):

> "It's not just the chest pain they're having in that moment that
> matters — it's their whole story, what led up to this moment."

> "All their prior history is relevant. What medications were they
> on, what side effects did they have, what surgeries took place and
> how did that affect them? It's a ton of work to go back and find
> all of that information during a time-sensitive case."

> "All of that medical history is crucial, but going in cold and
> sifting through that is a huge lift. Having ChatEHR boil that down
> into a relevant summary would make that process smoother."

---

## 8. Against hype (Beyond the Peak of Inflated Expectations, NEJM 2017)

Chen & Asch's foundational essay:

> "Machine learning now rides atop the 'peak of inflated
> expectations.'"

> "Precise predictions about the distant future are often fundamentally
> impossible."

> "The relevance of clinical data decays with an effective 'half-life'
> of about 4 months."

> "No amount of algorithmic finesse or computing power can squeeze
> out information that is not present."

> "Confounding makes it a substantial leap in causal inference to
> identify modifiable factors that will actually alter outcomes."

> "An accurate prediction of a patient outcome does not tell us what
> to do if we want to change that outcome."

> "Let's move past the hype cycle and on to the 'slope of
> enlightenment,' where we use every information and data resource
> to consistently improve our collective health."

---

## 9. AI winter caution

From HIMSS / Chief Healthcare Executive:

> "I'm at Stanford, an academic institution and one of the centers
> for AI in medicine ideas, and I'm hearing very smart people buying
> into ideas when they don't quite know what they're buying into.
> They're hearing promises that they're not going to follow up on,
> and I don't think they quite understand what those mean."

He explicitly links current hype to the 1980s AI winter, stem-cell
overpromising, and the Human Genome Project as historical yardsticks
for backlash risk.

---

## 10. "AI won't replace doctors; doctors who use AI may replace those who don't."

His labor-market pivot (HIMSS 2024):

> "A good human, a good doctor, you cannot replace them no matter
> how good a computer ever gets."

> "While AI is not going to replace doctors, those who learn how to
> use AI may very well replace those who do not."

> "It's becoming really hard to tell what is real and what is not
> nowadays."

---

## 11. On medical education in the AI era

From Stanford Medicine Magazine:

> "In 2022 when ChatGPT came out and blew up as the fastest-growing
> internet application in history, I thought, 'Whoa, this is like
> when the internet was invented.'"

> "Everything about education, especially with training and evaluating
> people based on what they read and write, just got turned upside
> down, and we have to rethink how to train new students and how to
> retrain an entire generation of the health care workforce."

> "Unlike how there is a curriculum for teaching how the kidney works,
> we cannot pin down a fixed curriculum for AI in health care because
> the technology is a moving target."

> "We need to have an adaptable framework. Luckily, many of the
> concepts needed for AI education are foundational, and we are
> already talking about them."

> "The algorithms can provide information that sounds correct but is
> actually wrong. The concepts of sensitivity and specificity can help
> students evaluate AI."

## 12. Humility on empathy — "Who's Training Whom?" (NEJM AI 2024)

His most vulnerable piece of public writing. Won the AAMC Fenley
Writing Award (Silver 2025).

> "The chatbot was likely providing better counseling than I did in
> real life."

> "Technology is leveraged to complement, not replace, the human
> elements of medicine."

> "We, as humans, may not have as much of a monopoly on empathy and
> personal connection as we might like to imagine."

## 13. Fountain of Creativity or Pandora's Box (NEJM AI 2024)

Three-tier framework for LLM impact on medicine:

1. **Text analysis, synthesis, documentation** (ambient scribes,
   chart biopsy)
2. **New care-delivery and patient-communication workflows**
3. **Re-drawing the human/AI expertise boundary**

**On confabulation (explicit naming):**
> LLMs are "prone to confabulation, assembling coherent strings of
> words into sentences that sound believable while being completely
> fabricated."

---

## 14. The opioid long-tail critique (JAMA IM 2016, with Shah)

Chen's computational-epidemiology move:
- Top 10% of opioid prescribers account for 57% of prescriptions.
- **But the same long-tail pattern holds for all drugs.**
- The "pill mill bad apples" framing of the opioid epidemic is wrong.
- **Most opioid prescriptions come from the broad base of general
  practitioners.**

This illustrates his range beyond ML-in-medicine. He wields big-data
on non-AI clinical questions.

## 17. His signature "no"

Things Chen refuses to endorse:

1. **Autonomous AI agents in medicine without reproducible real-task
   benchmarks.** MedAgentBench is the positive expression of this no.
2. **"LLM passes USMLE, therefore clinically ready."** *"Akin to
   assessing someone's driving ability using only a written test on
   traffic rules."*
3. **Patients skipping the doctor for ChatGPT.** "Don't do that."
4. **The 2017-present hype cycle** — "Beyond the Peak of Inflated
   Expectations."
5. **Retrospective-only evaluation** of high-stakes tools.
6. **The idea that humans monopolize empathy.** "Who's Training Whom"
   takes that comfort off the table.
7. **Final diagnostic prediction as the AI target.** Wayfinding paper
   rejects label-prediction framing.

### His rhetorical move
He doesn't attack people. He builds the measuring stick (the RCT, the
benchmark) and lets the data reframe the debate.
