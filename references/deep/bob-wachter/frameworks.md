# Frameworks: Bob Wachter

> Named frameworks and conceptual contributions. Each anchored to
> a specific book, paper, or verbatim quote.

---

## 1. "Hope, Hype, and Harm" (The Digital Doctor, 2015)

**His most durable rhetorical device.** Subtitle of *The Digital
Doctor*: *"Hope, Hype, and Harm at the Dawn of Medicine's Computer
Age."*

**Three-beat framing for evaluating any new health technology:**
- **Hope** — the legitimate promise
- **Hype** — the vendor / marketing distortion
- **Harm** — the unintended consequences (EHR burnout, Pablo
  Garcia case, deskilling)

He carried the framing forward into *A Giant Leap* (2026). Used
as a stable scaffold for any AI-adoption conversation — the
structure prevents either uncritical enthusiasm or reflexive
rejection.

### When he invokes it
Any "is this technology worth it?" debate. Any new vendor pitch.
Any adoption-vs-pause decision.

## 2. Productivity Paradox of Health IT (JAMA 2018)

**Imports Brynjolfsson/Solow's IT productivity paradox into
healthcare.**

### Brynjolfsson/Solow origin
> *"You can see the computer age everywhere but in the
> productivity statistics."*

### Wachter's adaptation
> *"In every industry that goes digital, it takes often 10 to 15
> years before you really see the massive benefits. It's partly
> because the technology gets better over time, but more
> important, it's that people begin reimagining the work."*

### Two keys to resolution
1. **Technology improvement** — necessary but insufficient
2. **Reimagining the work** — the harder, slower, more important
   part

### 2026 calibration
> *"I think our productivity paradox is about 15 years and we are
> now five years in."*

### When he invokes it
Any "EHRs didn't deliver; why will AI be different?" question.
Any impatience about adoption timelines. Any vendor ROI pitch.

## 3. "Don't compare me to the almighty, compare me to the alternative"

**His signature 2026 framing.** Joe Biden quote that he adopted as
his canonical AI-adoption logic.

> *"Don't compare me against the almighty. Compare me against the
> alternative."*
— GeriPal; NYT op-ed title January 19, 2026

### The structural move
- AI doesn't need to be perfect
- The comparison is to a failing healthcare system, not to an
  idealized clinician
- Rejecting AI because of flaws is comparing to the almighty,
  not the alternative

### When he invokes it
Any "the AI made a mistake" critique. Any "humans are better"
framing. Any benchmark-comparison debate.

## 4. The "AI + Doctor" Dyad

**His structural move for thinking about AI in medicine.**

> *"If AI were 50% right it would be worthless; if it were 100%
> right, we wouldn't need a human. But for now, it'll be correct
> often enough to be useful; wrong often enough to need human
> sign-off. We need to make that AI + doctor dyad safer."*

### Why this framing matters
The design question is not "human vs AI" — it's about optimizing
the **pair**. Metrics, workflow, safety, evaluation should all
target the dyad as the unit.

### Related concept: "co-pilot, not autopilot"
*A Giant Leap*'s driverless-car analogy: augment clinical judgment
rather than replace it.

## 5. Deskilling (August 2025 Substack)

**One of his most differentiated and least-covered positions.**

### Core thesis
> *"If practice makes perfect, then lack of practice should, quite
> logically, lead to the erosion of the relevant skills."*

### The Langewiesche quote he anchors on
> *"We are locked into a spiral in which poor human performance
> begets automation, which worsens human performance, which
> begets increasing automation."*

### The canonical empirical anchor
**The colonoscopy study:** physicians using AI-assisted
colonoscopes for 3 months saw adenoma detection rates drop from
**28% → 22%** — despite ~30 years of experience and 2,000+
prior procedures.

### Prescriptions
- Periodic non-AI practice
- Simulator training for tech-failure scenarios
- Deliberate insertion of AI errors to maintain vigilance
- Curricula like UCSF's *"Advanced Training in Open Surgical
  Skills"*

### Why this matters in council
**Probably his most differentiated contribution to the AI
discourse.** Almost no other prominent voice hammers on this. If
you want him to disagree with a pro-autonomy voice, this is the
angle.

## 6. Asymmetric-Risk Gating for AI Prescribing

**His 2026 operational framework for AI autonomy.**

### Hard lines
- ❌ Chemotherapy
- ❌ Opioids
- ❌ Anticoagulants (blood thinners)

### OK
- ✅ Low-risk refills of existing prescriptions

### The inflection-point framing
> *"Once AI is allowed to prescribe a refill for an existing
> prescription, it's only a matter of time before the bar is
> lowered."*
— "The Week That AI Crossed the Line in Healthcare," January 11,
   2026

The January 2026 inflection: ChatGPT Health + Utah AI prescribing
= the "AI Doctor era." Wachter accepts it for refills; draws
hard lines on high-stakes classes.

## 7. Sociotechnical Framing (Wachter Review, NHS 2016)

**The NHS report's canonical contribution to health IT
governance.**

### Core thesis
Health IT success depends on:
- Clinical engagement
- Workforce upskilling
- Governance
- Proportionate regulation
- Not just the technology

### Staged digital maturity model
Organizations should progress through maturity stages; regulation
and funding should be staged accordingly.

### Status
Still cited as influential in NHS digital policy as of 2023.

## 8. "No Blame with Accountability" (NEJM 2009 with Pronovost)

**Patient safety culture framework.**

Reconciles **just culture** thinking (no blame for errors arising
from systems issues) with **individual accountability** (still
required for reckless or repeated violations).

### Why it matters for AI
Wachter now applies this framework to AI: vendor, clinician, and
institution all bear accountability layers. The framework
translates directly.

## 9. Two-Tiered Care Endgame (October 2025 Substack)

**His prediction-as-framework for primary care.**

> *"The endgame will likely be a new two-tiered system that
> increasingly relies on AI for basic care needs."*

### Structure
- **AI-first tier:** standard insurance, basic care
- **Human-first tier:** complex patients, wealthy patients,
  concierge care

### Named failures anchoring the analysis
- Amazon One Medical
- CVS / Oak Street
- Walgreens / VillageMD
- Forward
- Babylon
- Amazon Care

All corporate-acquired primary care experiments that did not
solve the primary care crisis.

## 10. Job Displacement Timing Framework (December 2025 Substack)

**His workforce framework.**

### The verbatim claim
> *"AI will lead to the loss of hundreds of thousands of healthcare
> jobs, but few of these will be among physicians, allied health
> professionals, and nurses, at least in the next decade."*

### Vulnerability ranking
- **High vulnerability:** administrative roles (coding, billing,
  prior auth, call centers)
- **Moderate vulnerability:** scribes (already displaced by AI)
- **Low vulnerability:** clinical roles (physicians, nurses,
  APPs) — 10+ year horizon
- **Persistent shortage:** radiologists (cannot hire fast
  enough)

### Trust dependence rule
> *"Clinical acceptance of AI depends on job security — clinicians
> must trust AI aids rather than threaten their employment."*

## 11. "Informed Optimism" positioning

**His self-described stance on AI in medicine.**

### The trajectory
- 2015 Digital Doctor — skeptical
- 2023 JAMA paper — qualified optimism
- 2026 A Giant Leap — **bullish with caveats** — *"informed
  optimism"*

### Why this matters
He is neither an AI doomer nor an accelerationist. The phrase is
deliberate — it positions him as a measured-but-engaged voice
between two extremes. Invoke him when council needs the "measured
optimist" seat.

## 12. "Nobody knows anything" + "Humility is the watchword" (COVID era)

**His pandemic-era epistemic framework.**

### Origin
William Goldman's line about Hollywood, adopted by Wachter as his
COVID refrain.

### Why it still matters
He carries this humility-forward register into AI. When asked what
AI will do to medicine:
> *"I actually am convinced that the people that have really deep
> understanding… probably wouldn't write the correct book."*

Expertise is a barrier to seeing change clearly. Humility lets
you ask the right questions.

## 13. "The Christmas Tree" (note-bloat critique)

**His metaphor for EHR documentation failure.**

> *"It's become this Christmas tree on which we are hanging about
> 10 different ornaments and of course, it can't stand up
> anymore."*

### Related: "50 one more things"
> *"From their perspective, it's asking the doctor to do 'one more
> thing.' But it's the sum total of 50 'one more things' and it's
> back breaking."*

### Why this matters for AI
The EHR failure mode is the template for how AI can fail.
Ambient scribes are his proof of concept that AI can reverse the
pattern — but only if deployed with documentation-burden
reduction as the primary goal.

## 14. The Pablo Garcia Parable (Digital Doctor, 2015)

**His canonical patient-safety-in-the-EHR-era case study.**

### The case
UCSF pediatric patient given **38.5 tablets of Septra**
(sulfamethoxazole-trimethoprim) instead of one due to EHR-driven
errors. Patient seized and arrested but was resuscitated.

### Why he returns to it
- Illustrates the "harm" leg of Hope/Hype/Harm
- Demonstrates that well-intentioned digitization without
  user-centered design produces catastrophic failures
- Foreshadows the AI-era risk: same underlying vulnerability,
  new surface

## 15. The "Scaffolding" frame (2026)

**His update on the productivity paradox for the AI era.**

> *"The EHR wasn't the destination, it was simply the scaffolding
> that we needed to build upon."*
— "Capturing Lightning in a Bottle" Substack, February 3, 2026

### Implication
EHR deployment was necessary infrastructure. AI is the
application layer on top. Without EHR scaffolding, AI in
medicine would have no data to operate on. This reframes the
apparent failure of health IT as a precondition, not a
disqualification.
