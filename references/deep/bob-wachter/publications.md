# Publications: Bob Wachter

> 6 books. 300+ peer-reviewed papers per UCSF bio. Two flagship
> books anchor his public identity: *The Digital Doctor* (2015)
> and *A Giant Leap* (2026). Canonical clinical paper: the 1996
> NEJM hospitalist paper with Lee Goldman.

---

## Books — chronological

### 1. *The Fragile Coalition: Scientists, Activists and AIDS* (1991)
His earliest book, pre-hospitalist era. AIDS advocacy / public
health framing.

### 2. *Internal Bleeding: The Truth Behind America's Terrifying Epidemic of Medical Mistakes* (hardcover 2004; paperback 2005)
**Co-authored with Kaveh Shojania.** Published by Rugged Land.
Popular-audience patient safety book — one of the canonical
popular texts that brought patient safety into mainstream public
awareness.

### 3. *Hospital Medicine* (textbook, 2nd Edition 2005)
Multi-editor reference for the specialty he helped create.

### 4. *Understanding Patient Safety* — multiple editions (1st 2007, 2nd 2012, 3rd 2017/2018)
**3rd edition co-authored with Kiran Gupta.** Standard textbook
in the field. Primary sources disagree on 3rd-edition year
(2017 vs 2018).

### 5. *The Digital Doctor: Hope, Hype, and Harm at the Dawn of Medicine's Computer Age* (2015)
**New York Times bestseller.** McGraw-Hill Education.
ISBN 978-0071849463. Solo-authored. Based on ~100 interviews.

**Core themes:**
- Loss of patient-physician connection under EHR-mediated care
- Residents "glued" to screens
- Absence of user-centered design
- Productivity paradox of health IT
- Gap between digitization and value

**Pablo Garcia case** — the book's narrative spine. A UCSF
pediatric patient given **38.5 tablets of Septra
(sulfamethoxazole-trimethoprim) instead of one** due to
EHR-driven errors. Patient seized, arrested, was resuscitated. The
case is Wachter's canonical parable about poor EHR design.

**"Hope, Hype, and Harm"** — title-framing that has become his
durable rhetorical device. He explicitly carried it forward into
*A Giant Leap*.

### 6. *A Giant Leap: How AI Is Transforming Healthcare and What That Means for Our Future* (2026) — **FLAGSHIP AI BOOK**
- **Published:** February 3, 2026
- **Publisher:** Portfolio / Penguin Random House
- **Solo-authored**
- **Status:** instant national bestseller
- **Research basis:** 100+ interviews with pioneers across
  medicine, tech, policy, and business
- **Writing process:** Wachter used **Claude** for "idea
  generation and wordsmithing" (his own acknowledgment)
- **Blurb from Ethan Mollick:** *"The definitive account of AI's
  transformation of healthcare … Essential reading."*

**Core claims:**
- AI can deliver what digitization long promised but didn't
- AI is uniquely risky because it *seems* human
- Success will depend *"less on how good the tech is and more on
  issues like money, culture, governance, guilds, regulations,
  and history."*

**Signature framing:** AI as a **"co-pilot" (driverless-car
analogy)** rather than autonomous agent.

## Canonical academic papers

### 1. The Hospitalist paper — NEJM 1996
**Wachter RM, Goldman L.** *"The Emerging Role of 'Hospitalists'
in the American Health Care System."* NEJM 1996;335(7):514-517.
DOI: 10.1056/NEJM199608153350713. Published August 15, 1996.

**The founding document of hospital medicine.** Precise Scholar
citation count not verified from primary sources — flagged as
open.

### 2. Zero to 50,000 — NEJM 2016
**Wachter RM, Goldman L.** *"Zero to 50,000 — The 20th Anniversary
of the Hospitalist."* NEJM 2016;375:1009-1011. DOI:
10.1056/NEJMp1607958.

20-year retrospective. Hospital medicine grew from 0 → ~50,000
practitioners in two decades.

### 3. "Balancing 'No Blame' with Accountability in Patient Safety" — NEJM 2009
**Wachter RM, Pronovost PJ.** NEJM 2009;361:1401-1406. Patient
safety canon. Reconciles just-culture thinking with individual
accountability.

### 4. "Hospitalists in the United States — Mission Accomplished or Work in Progress?" — NEJM 2004
Mid-career hospitalist status update.

### 5. "Resolving the Productivity Paradox of Health Information Technology: A Time for Optimism" — JAMA 2018
**Wachter RM, Goldman L.** JAMA 2018;320(1):25-26.

Imports Brynjolfsson/Solow's "productivity paradox" into
healthcare. **Robert Solow's quip:** *"You can see the computer
age everywhere but in the productivity statistics."*

Argument: EHR adoption initially depressed rather than raised
productivity; the 15-year lag between digitization and value is
normal for general-purpose technologies.

### 6. "Will Generative Artificial Intelligence Deliver on Its Promise in Health Care?" — JAMA 2024 (online Nov 30, 2023)
**Wachter RM, Brynjolfsson E.** JAMA 2024;331(1):65-69.
DOI: 10.1001/jama.2023.25054. Published online Nov. 30, 2023 —
the **one-year anniversary of ChatGPT's public launch.**

Co-author is Erik Brynjolfsson of Stanford Digital Economy Lab /
HAI.

**Core argument:** generative AI's speed and self-corrective
capabilities will shorten the lag between promise and results vs.
prior health IT. Qualified optimism. **His most-cited recent AI
paper.**

### 7. "The Hospitalist Movement 25 Years Later" — Annual Review of Medicine 2023
25-year retrospective, extending the NEJM 2016 paper.

### 8. "Uptake of Generative AI Integrated With Electronic Health Records in US Hospitals" — co-authored 2026 paper
PubMed: 41385223. Exact author list unverified — flagged.

### 9. "Return on Investment for Clinician-Facing Artificial Intelligence Technologies"
Cited on his 2026 UCSF profile. Exact citation unverified.
