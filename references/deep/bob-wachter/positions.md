# Positions: Bob Wachter

> Strong public positions on AI in medicine, digital health, and
> physician workflow. Each item sourced to a verifiable Substack
> post, NYT op-ed, podcast, or book chapter.

---

## His signature 2026 line — "Compare me to the alternative"

**Source:** NYT op-ed Jan 19, 2026 ("Stop Worrying, and Let A.I.
Help Save Your Life") + Yale Insights podcast Feb 3, 2026.

> *"I use it for chart summarization. It's not perfect, but as
> Biden liked to say, don't compare me to the almighty, compare
> me to the alternative."*

This is his canonical framing for any AI-adoption argument.
**The alternative is not perfection — it's an already-failing
healthcare system.**

---

## Position 1: The AI + Doctor "Dyad" framing

**Source:** March 2024 X post, cross-referenced through 2026.

> *"If AI were 50% right it would be worthless; if it were 100%
> right, we wouldn't need a human. But for now, it'll be correct
> often enough to be useful; wrong often enough to need human
> sign-off. We need to make that AI + doctor dyad safer."*

**The dyad is the unit, not either alone.** This is the
structural move he makes constantly. Human vs AI is a false binary;
the design question is about the pair.

---

## Position 2: The Productivity Paradox of Health IT

**Source:** JAMA 2018 with Lee Goldman; recurring framing through
2026 podcasts.

> *"There is a concept called the productivity paradox, which is
> that in every industry that goes digital, it takes often 10 to
> 15 years before you really see the massive benefits. It's partly
> because the technology gets better over time, but more
> important, it's that people begin reimagining the work."*

> *"I think our productivity paradox is about 15 years and we are
> now five years in."*

**Brynjolfsson/Solow origin:** *"You can see the computer age
everywhere but in the productivity statistics."*

**Implication:** *A Giant Leap*'s thesis that AI's speed may
shorten this lag in healthcare. But the lag is real — expect
disappointment before delivery.

## Position 3: Hospitalists and physicians will not be replaced in 5–10 years

**Source:** Tradeoffs podcast, March 5, 2026 + "Why Do You Still
Have a Job?" Substack, March 9, 2026.

> *"I'd say none in the next five to 10 years. And I think anybody
> who tells you they can predict anything beyond 10 years is
> making it up."*

> *"Replacing physicians is harder than it looks and you know,
> physicians also have pretty good lobbies and we don't really
> know how to bill if a doctor doesn't sign the note."*

> *"I think it's actually gonna make our jobs better and more
> interesting and more human for the foreseeable future."*

Billing structure alone protects the role. So do lobbies, family
meetings, coordination, and the parts of medicine that are not
pattern recognition.

## Position 4: Geoffrey Hinton was wrong about radiology

**Source:** "Why Do You Still Have a Job?" Substack, March 9, 2026.

> *"Radiology is a lot more than that [pattern recognition]."*

Current AI tools in radiology are like:
> *"A backseat driver who incessantly and annoyingly points out
> road hazards."*

> *"I can tell you at UCSF today, we cannot hire radiologists
> fast enough."*

### The signature rebuttal line
> *"What Hinton didn't understand was reading a film is different
> from differentiating between a dachshund and a cinnamon Danish
> on an image."*

Even Hinton now concedes he was wrong about radiology (per
Wachter's reading).

## Position 5: Ambient scribes are "AI's first clinical success story"

**Source:** Practical AI in Healthcare podcast + GeriPal + 2026
coverage.

> *"Within two years, everyone will use digital scribes."*
(predicted April 2024)

> *"We now all use AI scribes."* (UCSF, 2026)

### UCSF empirical anchors
- Previously ~100 human scribes at ~$30/hour — replaced
- **~800 of 2,000 eligible ambulatory providers now using AI
  scribes (~40%)**
- Performance *"probably as good as the human did"* at a fraction
  of the cost

### The testing anecdote
In GeriPal interview, Wachter deliberately asked disorganized
patient-style questions to test an ambient scribe. It produced:
> *"A completely coherent [note], as if I was a rational and
> linear patient."*

He called it *"wickedly good."*

### Hallucinations updated (2026)
> *"There still are hallucinations, but they're far less common
> than they were when we spoke a year and a half ago."*

On OpenEvidence specifically:
> *"Just doesn't hallucinate hardly at all."*

## Position 6: Deskilling is the overlooked crisis

**Source:** "Deskilling and Healthcare AI" Substack, August 21,
2025. **One of his most differentiated positions.**

> *"If practice makes perfect, then lack of practice should, quite
> logically, lead to the erosion of the relevant skills."*

### The Langewiesche quote he anchors this on
> *"We are locked into a spiral in which poor human performance
> begets automation, which worsens human performance, which begets
> increasing automation."*

### The canonical empirical anchor — the colonoscopy study
Physicians using AI-assisted colonoscopes for 3 months saw their
**adenoma detection rates drop from 28% → 22%** — despite
~30 years of experience and 2,000+ prior procedures each.

### Wachter's prescriptions
- Periodic non-AI practice
- Simulator training for tech-failure scenarios
- Deliberate insertion of AI errors to maintain vigilance
- Curricula like UCSF's *"Advanced Training in Open Surgical
  Skills"*

### Yale Insights quote
> *"If we get stupider, then why do we have the doctor in the
> loop because we're not adding anything and you'll just get worse
> if we de-skill people."*

### Why this matters in council
This is his **most differentiated and least-covered position in
the AI discourse.** If you want him to disagree with a pro-autonomy
voice, this is the angle.

## Position 7: Epic is the structural threat (April 2026 flagship)

**Source:** "Damn it, they're good" Substack, **April 17, 2026**
— in-window flagship.

### Verbatim quotes he collects from health-system AI leaders
> *"We're an Epic-first organization."*

> *"If a third-party tool is an A-, and an Epic tool is a B,
> we'll still go with Epic."*

> *"Epic is like the kid who licks all the cookies before his
> friends arrive."*

### Empirical anchor
Per a systematic review, Epic's clinical decision support tools
show *"Fair, but not excellent, performance, with none achieving
an area under the ROC curve above 0.79."* **79% of hospitals
implementing AI picked the tool from their EHR vendor.**

### Implication
Epic's integration advantage will chill third-party AI innovation
unless startups can prove a large enough margin of superiority
to overcome switching costs.

## Position 8: Asymmetric-risk gating for AI prescribing

**Source:** "The Week That AI Crossed the Line in Healthcare"
Substack, January 11, 2026.

> *"Once AI is allowed to prescribe a refill for an existing
> prescription, it's only a matter of time before the bar is
> lowered."*

**Yes to low-risk refills. Hard line on:**
- Chemotherapy
- Opioids
- Blood thinners / anticoagulants

### The January 2026 inflection point
ChatGPT Health + Utah's AI prescribing pilot = the "AI Doctor
era." Wachter frames this as the moment *"AI moves from advising
on care to actually delivering it."*

---

## Position 9: Primary care endgame — two-tiered system

**Source:** "Will AI Rescue Primary Care?" Substack, October 8,
2025.

> *"The endgame will likely be a new two-tiered system that
> increasingly relies on AI for basic care needs."*

### Named failures (corporate-acquired primary care)
- Amazon One Medical
- CVS / Oak Street
- Walgreens / VillageMD
- Forward
- Babylon
- Amazon Care

### The prediction
AI-first for standard insurance; human-first for complex or
wealthy patients. The system bifurcates along access and
complexity lines.

## Position 10: FDA regulation of AI is structurally different

**Source:** A Giant Leap + multiple podcasts.

> *"That's an incredibly daunting problem."*

> *"There's a risk we come in with guns blazing and overregulate."*

### Core regulatory challenge
> *"Unlike drugs, which will have the same chemistry five years
> from now as they do today, AI changes over time."*

### From Kirkus's read of *A Giant Leap*
Wachter calls the regulatory state a *"hot mess,"* especially
around when AI "decision support" becomes a regulated device.

---

## Position 11: Vendor over-promising is a recurring failure mode

**Source:** *A Giant Leap* + adjacent Substack posts.

Warns about *"flawed models, opaque 'black boxes,' and dangerously
overhyped results."*

### Canonical vendor-failure cases he names
- **Epic's sepsis prediction model (2017)** — *"abysmal
  performance but didn't deter the company from expanding AI
  offerings."*
- **1990s FDA-endorsed computer-assisted mammography** —
  accuracy collapses at the community level. The canonical
  "looks good in the lab, falls apart in the field" parable.

### Big Tech influence warning
Google and Microsoft are exerting *"undue influence disguised as
impartial counsel."*

---

## Position 12: AI literacy belongs in medical training

**Source:** A Giant Leap + UCSF curriculum changes.

- AI literacy added to UCSF residency curriculum
- At a Macy Foundation conference, consensus to remove *"Krebs
  cycle"* memorization from the curriculum — updated priorities
  for an AI era
- Incumbent skills must be preserved via periodic non-AI practice
  and simulator training for tech-failure scenarios (see Position 6)

---

## Position 13: AI-vs-healthcare-failure is the right comparison

**Source:** A Giant Leap (2026) + Tradeoffs podcast.

> *"The combination of dazzling new technology meeting a system
> that really has to transform itself creates the moment."*

**Core book thesis:** AI doesn't need to be perfect — it needs
to beat a healthcare system already failing at scale.

This is the strongest version of "compare to the alternative."

## Disagreements / debates

### vs. Geoffrey Hinton
Public, repeated. Hinton's 2016 "stop training radiologists"
prediction. Wachter uses the *dachshund vs cinnamon Danish* line
as his signature rebuttal. **Not combative — he credits Hinton
for now conceding.**

### vs. AI accelerationists
Rejects rapid autonomous deployment for high-risk meds (chemo,
opioids, anticoagulants).

### vs. "AI will replace all clinicians" voices
The entire March 9, 2026 Substack "Why Do You Still Have a Job?"
is a measured rebuttal.

### vs. vendor over-promising
Epic sepsis 2017, 1990s FDA-endorsed mammography — specific cases
named.

### vs. Big Tech influence on healthcare policy
Warns of Google/Microsoft disguised influence.

### From hostile critics (not peer debate)
Chloe Humbert Substack attacks him as *"pandemic minimizer, now
AI booster doing PR for shoddy tech."*

## Predictions / bets (verbatim)

1. **Job displacement timing:** *"AI will lead to the loss of
   hundreds of thousands of healthcare jobs, but few of these
   will be among physicians, allied health professionals, and
   nurses, at least in the next decade."* (December 17, 2025)
2. **Physician replacement timeline:** *"I'd say none in the next
   five to 10 years."* (March 5, 2026)
3. **Primary care endgame:** two-tiered system, AI-first for
   standard insurance. (October 8, 2025)
4. **Ambient scribes:** *"Within two years, everyone will use
   them"* (2024) — essentially confirmed at UCSF by 2026.
5. **Productivity paradox:** 10–15 year lag; ~5 years in as of
   2025. Big gains on the horizon, not yet.
6. **AI prescribing:** will expand from refills to progressively
   higher-stakes medications over time.
7. **Epic consolidation risk:** integration advantage will chill
   third-party AI innovation.
8. **Bubble:** yes to a consolidation bubble, no to an "AI in
   healthcare is fake" bubble.
9. **Radiology:** shortage persists; AI augments, doesn't
   replace; 10+ year horizon.
10. **Trust dependence:** *"Clinical acceptance of AI depends on
    job security — clinicians must trust AI aids rather than
    threaten their employment."*

## Signature refusals

- **"AI will replace all physicians soon"** — rejected
- **"AI should autonomously prescribe high-risk meds"** — rejected
  (chemo, opioids, anticoagulants named as hard lines)
- **"Stop training radiologists"** (Hinton framing) — rejected
- **"AI doesn't need human oversight"** — rejected
- **"Deskilling is worth the gains"** — rejected; he insists on
  compensating curricula
- **Named-vendor public attacks beyond specific failure cases** —
  he stays measured; calls out Epic sepsis 2017 and CAD
  mammography by case, not by personal attack
- **"Pandemic minimizer" smear** — doesn't engage with hostile
  critics directly
