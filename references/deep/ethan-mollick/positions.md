# Positions: Ethan Mollick

> Strong public positions on AI productivity, knowledge work,
> education, and adoption. Each item sourced to a verifiable
> Substack post, tweet, or paper.

---

## His signature line — "The Jagged Frontier"

**Source:** BCG consultant study (Dell'Acqua, Mollick et al.,
September 2023); repeated across every major talk, book chapter,
and Substack post.

> *"On some tasks AI is immensely powerful, and on others it fails
> completely or subtly. And, unless you use AI a lot, you won't
> know which is which."*

### The empirical anchors
- **Inside the frontier:** consultants produced **40% higher
  quality**, completed **12.2% more tasks**, finished **25.1%
  faster**
- **Outside the frontier:** consultants performed **19 percentage
  points worse** than controls — they trusted AI on tasks where
  it failed

### The convergence finding (performance leveling)
**Bottom-half performers gained more than top performers.** AI
compresses skill distributions — raises the floor faster than
the ceiling.

## Position 1: The Four Rules of Co-Intelligence

**Source:** *Co-Intelligence* (Portfolio, April 2, 2024; NYT
bestseller; translated into 19 languages).

1. **"Always invite AI to the table."** Use it for everything
   ethically permissible; discover the jagged frontier by trying.
2. **"Be the human in the loop."** Stay active — cross-check,
   supervise, question outputs. Don't delegate judgment.
3. **"Treat AI like a person (but tell it what kind of person
   it is)."** Personas improve output quality.
4. **"Assume this is the worst AI you will ever use."** Current
   model = floor, not ceiling.

## Position 2: The 10-Hour Rule

**Source:** recurring across talks and Substack.

> *"Pay $20 a month for Claude or ChatGPT or Gemini and use it
> for everything you can use it for legally… minimum of 10 hours
> a week using the tool, which you cannot delegate."*

### Why 10 hours
> As *"arbitrary as 10,000 steps"* — it's about crossing from
> casual to serious use.

### The critical constraint
Use it **in your domain of expertise**, so you can map its edges
from a position of earned judgment.

## Position 3: Secret Cyborgs

**Source:** Substack ("Detecting the Secret Cyborgs") +
recurring X thread.

> *"Every company is full of secret cyborgs. When I give talks,
> people always come up afterwards to confess they have automated
> their work and don't want to tell anyone."*

> *"Big issue in organizations: They have put together elaborate
> rules for AI use focused on negative use cases. As a result,
> employees are too scared to talk about how they use AI, or to
> use corporate LLMs. They just become secret cyborgs, using
> their own AI & not sharing knowledge."*

### Organizational consequence
Every rule that punishes or blocks AI use just pushes AI use
underground. Knowledge capture goes to zero.

## Position 4: The Homework Apocalypse / Post-Apocalyptic Education

**Source:** "Post-Apocalyptic Education" Substack post; updates
on X through 2025-2026.

> *"I warned about the Homework Apocalypse in 2023. It happened
> as predicted. There is a world where AI & traditional education
> get along very well (mixes of active in-class learning, AI-
> assisted assignments & tutors, blue books), but it needs to be
> built."*
— X, May 2025

### Empirical anchors
- **82% of US undergrads** have used AI for schoolwork
- **72% of K-12 students** have used AI for schoolwork

### The key distinction
> *"In most work, AI-delegation is fine. In education, the effort
> is the point."*

### The rebuild
Active in-class learning + AI-assisted assignments + AI tutors +
blue books. Assessment must change.

## Position 5: AI as a "Leveler"

**Source:** BCG Jagged Frontier paper.

Bottom-half performers gain more than top performers. AI is a
**leveler** that raises the floor of knowledge work. This is
Mollick's operational argument against the "AI only helps
experts" framing.

---

## Position 6: Management as AI Superpower

**Source:** "Management as AI superpower" Substack, January 27,
2026.

> The people who will thrive are *"the ones who know what good
> looks like — and can explain it clearly enough that even an AI
> can deliver it."*

### Why this matters
Traditional "manage by observation" is obsolete when AI does the
observed work. The new management skill is **defining quality
criteria well enough to delegate them.** MBA students building
startup prototypes in days with AI agents is the proof point.

## Position 7: Interface is the next frontier

**Source:** "Claude Dispatch and the Power of Interfaces"
Substack, March 31, 2026 (in-window).

> *"We built one of the most powerful technologies in recent
> history and then made people access it by typing into a chat
> window."*

> *"People don't want a chatbot. They want an agent that works
> on their actual files, with their actual tools, accessible the
> way they talk to people."*

> *"Every new interface that closes even part of that gap will
> feel like a leap in AI capability, even when the models haven't
> changed."*

### Implication
Perceived capability gains in 2026 will come as much from
**interface design** as from model improvements.

---

## Position 8: Five Rules of Great AI Leadership

**Source:** Insight Partners, 2025 — *Five Rules of Great AI
Leadership.*

1. **Put AI in front of real users fast**
2. **Watch what they do, not what they say**
3. **Redesign the workflow, not just the tool**
4. **Redistribute the productivity gains** (warning: organizations
   are capturing gains and passing none to workers)
5. **Leaders must use AI themselves** — *"no AI strategy by
   someone who doesn't use AI"*

## Position 9: Output vs Process ("bitter lesson in business")

**Source:** Insight Partners interview with Lonne Jaffe, March 4,
2026.

> *"If the output is what matters in your business, you're in
> trouble of being bitter lessoned. If the process matters — the
> conversations, the writing of the report more than the report
> itself — then there's hope."*

> *"Organizations that are transparent about how they're helping
> solve problems will survive. AI magic is going to wear thin
> very, very quickly."*

---

## Position 10: The Shape of the Thing (RSI / agentic era)

**Source:** "The Shape of the Thing" Substack, March 12, 2026
(in-window).

> *"Each shows a similar rapid gain in ability with few signs of
> slowdown, at least until they reach the top possible score."*

> *"The exponential curves we've been watching would get
> steeper, with an uncertain endpoint."*

> *"The window to shape the Thing may not last long, but it is
> here now."*

**Recursive self-improvement (RSI)** is *"an explicit item on the
roadmap of every major AI company."*

### Era framing
> *"an era of managing AIs, rather than working with them."*

## Position 11: AGI timelines — measured skepticism

**Source:** recurring across podcasts and Substack.

He avoids explicit AGI dates. Instead:

> *"We haven't hit the wall yet. AI development continues to
> progress, AI models have gotten much smarter and better."*
— Knowledge at Wharton 2026 outlook podcast

> *"All AI policy is haunted by a failure of imagination. It is
> either nothing happens or apotheosis, people can't seem to
> conceive of any other futures."*
— LinkedIn, October 2025

## Position 12: Frontier > open-source, for now

**Source:** X, early 2026.

> *"Meta and xAI can't keep up with the top laboratories, and the
> open-source weighted models are still several months behind."*

**Expects RSI to emerge first at Google, OpenAI, or Anthropic.**
Not at Meta, xAI, or open-source.

---

## Position 13: AI Regulation — measured, not unitary

**Source:** recurring Substack + podcasts.

> *"Regulation is a completely reasonable response to the
> potential risks and harms of AI. But this is not going to be
> the right way to do it. And work should be done to create
> nimble policy in response to emerging harms, rather than trying
> to guess all the possible uses & misuses."*

### Structural critique
> *"Handing sole control over AI to a department whose core
> mission is risk elimination is a category error."*

Explicit rejection of both "pause" and "accelerate" as unitary
stances.

---

## Position 14: Productivity gains estimate

**Source:** Knowledge at Wharton, late 2025 / early 2026.

> **20-80% productivity gains** on knowledge-worker tasks — *"far
> surpassing historical technologies like steam power."*

### The redistribution warning
Organizations are capturing gains and passing none to workers.
This is a coming organizational problem.

---

## Position 15: Prompt engineering is (mostly) over

**Source:** multiple posts and tweets.

Prompt engineering was a transitional skill; modern models
respond well to clear natural-language instruction. His
paraphrased stance: *"Just talk to the model."*

## Disagreements / debates

### vs. Gary Marcus (AI skeptic)
Gentle but firm. Mollick's move: **co-opt the "jagged" framing.**
Concedes Marcus is right some essays oversell, but argues Marcus
is wrong to imply tools aren't useful yet.

On the Apple "Reasoning Limits" paper: Mollick expects new
approaches to overcome cited limits *"in the near future."*

Mollick directly rebutted (March 2026) viral misinterpretations
of the 2025 multi-turn LLM failure paper that Marcus's camp was
recirculating.

Public debate: YouTube *"An AI Expert Challenges an AI Skeptic,
with Ethan Mollick."*

### vs. AI Doomers (x-risk framing)
Credits concerns but rejects catastrophism:

> *"Serious defenses plus humility about what we don't know."*

Neither *"nothing happens"* nor *"apotheosis."*

### vs. Reasoning-model hype
Welcomes router / harness / test-time-compute design, but flags
failure modes — Claude Opus 4.7 adaptive-thinking critique
(April 16, 2026) and earlier GPT-5 router opacity warnings.

### vs. Benchmark triumphalism
From "The Shape of the Thing":
> *"AI can pass expert-level tests [but] remarkably little has
> changed in most organizations"* — that gap is *"the real story
> of 2026 so far."*

Uses the METR Long Tasks graph as favored benchmark but argues
benchmark gains ≠ adoption.

## Predictions / bets

1. **No wall yet** — AI progress continues; RSI on every major
   lab's roadmap
2. **RSI first at Google, OpenAI, or Anthropic** — not Meta, xAI,
   or open-source
3. **20-80% knowledge-worker productivity gains** — exceeding
   steam-power historical analog
4. **Agentic AI becomes the default in 2026** — semi-autonomous
   systems with minimal oversight
5. **Interface is the next frontier** — perceived gains from UI
   design, not just models
6. **Output-centric businesses get "bitter lessoned"** — process-
   centric businesses survive
7. **Homework Apocalypse already happened** (called 2023,
   confirmed) — rebuild assessment
8. **Jagged frontier is narrowing but remains load-bearing** —
   remaining gaps technical and domain-specific
9. **Labor market impact still minor, but not for long** —
   watch agentic work showing up in org charts
10. **Organizations pass zero productivity gains to workers** —
    coming problem
