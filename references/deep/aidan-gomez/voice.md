# Voice: Aidan Gomez

> Voice fingerprint. Every quote below is sourced to a specific
> podcast, interview, X post, or blog.

---

## Cadence

**Dry, confident, Canadian-polite with a serrated edge.** Frames
himself as **the grown-up in the room** vs. hype. Measured
technical operator voice. Anti-OpenAI tone without naming OpenAI.
**Understatement and anti-hype are structural.**

### Signature register tells
- *"Prosaic"* — his word for the real AI revolution
- *"Quietly do good work"*
- *"We just want to…"*
- *"So I can't wait."* — closing beat

---

## Verbal tics and rhetorical moves

### Structural
- **Contrast-as-definition:** *"That's the difference between X
  and Y."* / *"It's not an 'either or', it's an 'and'."*
- **Hedged affirmative:** concedes the obvious before pivoting to
  his actual view — *"It's definitely true that… It's also the
  dumbest."*
- **"What I really care about…"** — redirects away from hype
  toward productivity / enterprise
- **Understatement / anti-hype:** *"We don't do these blockbuster
  launch event type things."*
- **Operator verbs:** deploy, plug in, production workloads,
  capture, flip (POCs into production), ship
- **Short declarative closers:** *"That's where the money is.
  That's where the impact is."*

### Signature openings (pattern)
- *"What I really care about is…"*
- *"There's this very obvious next step…"*
- *"Pretty much all of the major gains we've seen have come
  from…"*
- *"The status quo with models is…"*

### Signature closings
- *"So I can't wait."*
- *"That's what we're here for."*

> worked. We didn't know it would become the architecture for
> everything."*
— recalled in later interview (via digidai analysis)

> *"I ran hundreds of experiments testing different
> configurations. We were trying to figure out: how many

— 2024 podcast interview

> *"The transformer gave us the architecture. But architecture
> alone doesn't create value. You need to build products people

> consistently over years. That's what Cohere is — the
> transformer idea translated into enterprise infrastructure."*
— 2025 interview

### On scaling, models, data

> *"It's definitely true that if you throw more compute at the
> model, if you make the model bigger, it'll get better. It's
> kind of like it's the most trustworthy way to improve models.
> It's also the dumbest […] I just think it's extremely
> inefficient. There are much better ways."*
— 20VC, August 2024

> *"We live in this world of unbundled verticalized models,
> which are much more efficient and smaller, designed for
> specific use cases […] There will be both [bigger and smaller
> models]."*
— 20VC, August 2024

> *"Pretty much all of the major gains that we've seen in open
> source space have come from data improvements."*
— 20VC, August 2024

> *"The status quo with models is: I ask you a question and the
> model is expected to respond immediately with the right
> answer. That's an incredibly high burden to place on the
> model […] There's this very obvious next step for models,
> which is you need to let them think and work through
> problems."*
— 20VC, August 2024

> *"Anyone who has tried having a voice-based conversation with
> one of these models, it's a stunning experience."*
— 20VC, August 2024

> *"I think robotics is the place where there will be big
> breakthroughs."*
— 20VC, August 2024

### On hallucination and RAG

> *"Hallucination, which is when these models dream up facts.
> And one interesting point about that is that it's actually
> difficult to dream up facts, and the models don't want to do
> it… they'd much prefer to just offer that correct answer to
> the input. They only hallucinate when they feel like they
> have to. When they don't have access to that information.
> That's why RAG is so crucial."*
— Oracle interview, 2024

> *"RAG fundamentally changes the economics of enterprise AI.
> You don't need a $100 million training run every time your
> product catalog changes. You just update your database, and
> the model retrieves the latest information. That's the
> difference between AI that's practical and AI that's
> prohibitively expensive."*
— Cohere annual conference 2023
