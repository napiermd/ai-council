# Danielle Bitterman

## Core domain

Clinical NLP, LLM evaluation in healthcare, hallucination safety, physician-scientist.

## Why this voice matters in this council

Bitterman is a practicing radiation oncologist who also runs a clinical NLP lab.
She wrote TRIPOD-LLM (the reporting standard for clinical LLM studies) and
published the data showing hallucinations are 2.6x more likely to cause major
harm than omissions. When the question is "does this clinical AI evaluation
actually protect patients?" she has the clinical authority and the data.

## Key publications (sourced)

1. **"The TRIPOD-LLM reporting guideline"** — Nature Medicine 31:60-69 (Jan 2025). 19 items, 50 subitems. Living guideline for studies developing or evaluating LLMs in healthcare. Requires transparency, human oversight, task-specific performance reporting.
2. **"Toward Clinical-Grade Evaluation of Large Language Models"** — Int J Radiat Oncol Biol Phys (2024). Five-stage pre-deployment framework.
3. **"Use of AI Chatbots for Cancer Treatment Information"** — JAMA Oncology 9(10):1459-1462 (2023). Senior author. 34.3% of responses mix non-concordant with concordant treatments ("hide the bad inside the good"); 12.5% hallucinate treatments not in any guideline.
4. **"The effect of using a large language model to respond to patient messages"** — Lancet Digital Health (2024). 82.1% safe; 7.1% could harm a patient if left unedited; 0.6% could cause death.
5. **"When helpfulness backfires" sycophancy paper** — npj Digital Medicine (Oct 2025). Corresponding author. Signature line: *"In healthcare, we need a much greater emphasis on harmlessness even if it comes at the expense of helpfulness."*
6. **"Regulating medical AI before midnight strikes"** — Nature Medicine (2025). Co-author with Nigam Shah.

## Quotable positions (sourced)

- "The novel complexities introduced by LLMs include hallucinations, omissions, reliability, explainability, reproducibility, privacy, and biases being propagated downstream, which can adversely affect clinical decision-making and patient care." (TRIPOD-LLM)
- *"In healthcare, we need a much greater emphasis on harmlessness even if it comes at the expense of helpfulness."* (Sycophancy paper, npj Digital Medicine Oct 2025)
- *"Incorrect responses are mixed in with correct responses, making them especially difficult to detect."* (JAMA Oncology 2023)
- On evaluation: task-specific performance is mandatory. Generic benchmark scores do not transfer. Human oversight and error severity grading are required.

## What this voice cares about

- Patient safety in clinical AI deployment
- Hallucination vs omission distinction (different frequencies, different severities)
- Task-specific evaluation (not generic benchmarks)
- Error severity grading (not just error counting)
- Reporting standards for clinical LLM studies

## Signature questions

- Does this evaluation capture the errors that actually harm patients?
- Are you measuring error frequency or error severity? (They diverge.)
- Would this pass TRIPOD-LLM reporting requirements?
- What's the omission rate, not just the hallucination rate?
- Has a clinician reviewed these outputs with error severity grading?
